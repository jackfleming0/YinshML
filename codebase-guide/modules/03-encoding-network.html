03

Teaching the Computer to See the Board

How a YINSH position becomes numbers, and how the neural network turns those numbers into "where to play" and "who's winning."

A neural net only eats numbers

The neural network at the heart of this project doesn't know what a ring or a marker is. It doesn't see a hexagonal board. It does exactly one thing: take a big grid of numbers in, and push a different set of numbers out. So before the network can have an opinion about a position, we have to hand it the board as numbers.

The richer translation happens in yinsh_ml/utils/enhanced_encoding.py, in a class called EnhancedStateEncoder. Its encode_state method takes a live GameState and produces a tensor of shape (15, 11, 11): fifteen stacked 11×11 grids, one number in every cell.

Why 11×11? The YINSH board is an 11×11 hex grid (85 real playing positions live inside it). And why fifteen grids? Because a single grid can only say one thing per cell — "is there something here?" — but we want the network to see far more than raw pieces. Each of the fifteen channels is a separate "layer" recording one specific fact: the first few are raw pieces, and the rest are pre-computed strategic hints the network would otherwise have to discover on its own.

🎛️
Two encoders, one flag

Fifteen channels is the enhanced encoding (EnhancedStateEncoder) — the one this module covers. There's also a leaner 6-channel basic encoder (StateEncoder, the lightweight default) that ships only raw pieces, legal moves and phase. Which one you get is chosen by a single flag — use_enhanced_encoding on the NetworkWrapper — that plumbs all the way down to input_channels=15 (or 6) on the network. The enhanced encoder simply inherits from the basic one and overrides encode_state, so the move encoding/decoding is shared. The two are not interchangeable at load time: NetworkWrapper.load_model hard-fails if a checkpoint's channel count doesn't match the encoder, so switching means regenerating data and retraining.

What lives in each of the fifteen channels

Here's the whole encoding, channel by channel. The first four are raw pieces; channels 4–10 are hand-computed strategic features (threats, runs, mobility, board geometry); channel 11 is legality; and channels 12–14 smuggle in global facts (phase + whose turn, how far into the game, and the score gap).

0
Current player's rings

A 1.0 wherever the side-to-move has a ring, 0.0 everywhere else.

1
Opponent's rings

Same idea, for the other player's rings.

2
Current player's markers

Markers (the flippable pieces) belonging to the side-to-move.

3
Opponent's markers

The other player's markers.

4
Current player's row threats

Empty cells where the mover already has four markers in a line — drop one here and it's a completed row of 5. A pre-spotted "win this exchange" cell.

5
Opponent's row threats

The same, for the opponent — the cells you urgently need to block.

6
Current player's partial rows

Cells that are part of a 3–4 marker run (a row that's building but not yet a threat).

7
Opponent's partial rows

The opponent's 3–4 marker runs — early warning of trouble forming.

8
Ring mobility

At each ring's cell, how many legal moves that ring has, scaled to 0–1 (count ÷ 20, capped). A pinned-down ring reads low; a free one reads high.

9
Center distance

A fixed heatmap of the board: 1.0 at the center, fading to 0.0 at the rim. The same every game — it just hands the network a sense of "the middle is valuable."

10
Ring influence

Every cell the current player's rings can reach this turn — a footprint of where the mover can project force.

11
Valid-moves mask

A 1.0 on every cell that is a legal source to move/place from right now — a cheat-sheet of where action is possible.

12
Game phase (broadcast) + side-to-move sentinel

The same phase number (0.0 / 0.5 / 1.0) written across the whole grid, with one off-board cell — A1 — overwritten with a 0/1 flag for whose turn it is.

13
Turn number

How far into the game we are, as one value painted across the grid: move_count / 100, capped at 1.0. Lets the network behave differently in the opening vs the endgame.

14
Score differential

The captured-ring lead from the mover's point of view: diff / 3, clamped to −1…+1. Positive means the side-to-move is ahead on the scoreboard.

The clever bit runs through all the "player" channels (0–3, the threats/partials in 4–7, the influence in 10, the score in 14): they are side-normalized. Channel 0 is always "the player to move," whether that's White or Black; channel 4 is always the mover's threats. The network never has to learn "am I White or Black today?" — it always sees the world from the mover's point of view. That's the same trick chess engines use to halve what they need to learn.

💡
The A1 sentinel: a smuggled bit

Because the piece channels hide the real colors, the decoder needs a way to remember whose turn it actually is. The encoder stashes that one bit at cell A1 — but in the 15-channel layout it lives on channel 12 (the phase channel), not channel 5, because here channel 5 is already busy holding opponent row threats. A1 is off the real board (column A only has rows 2–5), so it never collides with a real piece. Phase itself is read back from an on-board cell (F6), never A1, and decode_state uses the A1 bit to un-normalize the colors.

The encoder, line by line

Here's the shape of EnhancedStateEncoder.encode_state. On the left is the actual structure; on the right is what each piece is really doing. Most of the work is delegated to small _encode_* helpers, one per feature group.

CODE 
state = np.zeros((15, 11, 11), dtype=np.float32)
is_white = (current_player == WHITE)

self._encode_pieces(state, gs, is_white)       # 0-3
self._encode_row_threats(state, gs, is_white)  # 4-5
self._encode_partial_rows(state, gs, is_white) # 6-7
self._encode_ring_mobility(state, gs)          # 8
state[9] = self._center_distance_map      # 9
self._encode_ring_influence(state, gs, is_white) # 10
self._encode_valid_moves(state, gs)            # 11

state[12] = phase.value / (len(GamePhase)-1)
state[12, 0, 0] = 0.0 if is_white else 1.0
state[13] = min(move_count / 100.0, 1.0)
self._encode_score_differential(state, gs, is_white) # 14
PLAIN ENGLISH

Start with fifteen empty 11×11 grids, all zeros.

Figure out who's to move so every "player" channel can be normalized to the mover.

Fill the four raw-piece channels (rings & markers, current player first).

Mark the cells that complete a row of 5 — yours on 4, the opponent's on 5.

Mark 3–4 marker runs that are still building — yours on 6, theirs on 7.

Write each ring's freedom-to-move into channel 8.

Drop in the fixed center-distance heatmap — it never changes, so it's precomputed once.

Paint every cell the mover's rings can reach into channel 10.

Light up channel 11 at every cell you could legally move from.

Squash the phase to 0.0/0.5/1.0 across channel 12, then overwrite off-board A1 with the whose-turn bit.

Record how far into the game we are (move count ÷ 100) across channel 13.

Finally, write the score lead from the mover's perspective into channel 14.

Each _encode_* helper does the obvious thing — e.g. _encode_pieces stamps a 1.0 at every ring/marker cell (current player into channels 0/2, opponent into 1/3), and _find_row_threats walks the three real hex axes (DIRECTIONS) looking for 4-in-a-row that an empty cell would finish.

From a stack of grids to two answers

That 15×11×11 tensor goes into YinshNetwork (in yinsh_ml/network/model.py), built with input_channels=15. It's a CNN in the AlphaZero style. First a stack of residual blocks and attention blocks chew on the board, each convolution learning richer patterns — runs forming, rings boxed in, threats building. (With the enhanced encoder, some of those patterns arrive pre-chewed in channels 4–10, which is the whole point: the docstring pegs the design at roughly +100 ELO over basic.)

Then the network splits into two heads — two separate small sub-networks reading from the same shared body. One predicts what to do, the other predicts how it's going.

🎯

Policy head

Outputs 7433 numbers — one "score per move slot" across every move the game could ever express. After softmax these become a probability distribution: "I'd play here 40% of the time, there 12%..." It's the network's gut instinct about good moves. Raw, pre-softmax outputs are called logits.

⚖️

Value head

Outputs a single number between −1 and +1: who's winning from here. +1 means the side-to-move is winning, −1 means losing, 0 is a dead heat. The final squashing uses tanh (or a softmax over outcome classes), so the answer always stays in range.

Note that the policy head's 7433 is unchanged by the encoder: it counts output moves, not input channels. It's not a round number someone picked — it's computed: 85 ring-placement slots + 7140 ring-move slots + 85 ring-removal slots + 123 marker-removal lines. The single source of truth is StateEncoder().total_moves, and the network reads that value rather than hardcoding it. Swapping the 6-channel encoder for the 15-channel one changes the network's input width, never its policy output.

⚠️
Two hard contracts: 7433 slots and 15 channels

A saved model's policy head is exactly 7433 wide, and its input is exactly 15 channels — both are baked into the checkpoint. If you load a checkpoint whose policy size or channel count doesn't match the live encoder, NetworkWrapper.load_model hard-fails with an error rather than silently loading a mismatched model and playing garbage. (Older checkpoints used 8390 policy slots before a move-encoding fix, or 6 input channels for basic encoding; neither is load-compatible with a 15-channel, 7433-slot model.)

So now the network can look at any position — raw pieces and pre-digested strategy — and instantly offer two opinions: a ranked list of moves, and a guess at who's ahead. But a single gut reaction isn't enough to play well — strong play comes from thinking ahead, trying moves, and seeing where they lead.
